Natural Honey

Chinese honey is available in an extensive range of floral varieties, including acacia, linden, jujube, buckwheat, vitex, and multifloral blends. Each variety offers a unique flavor profile, color, and nutritional composition. China exports both raw (unfiltered) honey and processed honey that has been filtered, pasteurized, and standardized for moisture content and HMF (hydroxymethylfurfural) levels. Bulk honey is typically shipped in 290kg steel drums, while retail-ready packaging is also available from many suppliers. Organic honey, certified to EU and USDA NOP standards, is increasingly in demand and readily available from certified Chinese producers.

Royal Jelly

Royal jelly is one of the most valued bee products in the global health and wellness market. Chinese suppliers offer both fresh royal jelly (shipped frozen at -18°C, MOQ 100kg by air) and royal jelly powder (lyophilized freeze-dried powder, room temperature stable, MOQ as low as 30kg via DHL). Fresh royal jelly contains approximately 60-70% moisture, 12-15% proteins, and the key quality marker 10-HDA (10-hydroxy-2-decenoic acid) at 1.4% or higher for premium grade. Royal jelly powder concentrates these active compounds and is preferred by buyers who need easier handling, longer shelf stability, and lower minimum orders. For a detailed guide on importing royal jelly specifically, including quality testing, shipping options, and supplier verification, see our dedicated Royal Jelly Import Guide →

Propolis

Chinese propolis is renowned for its high flavonoid content and potent bioactive properties. It is exported in several forms: raw propolis blocks (unrefined, as collected from hives), refined propolis extract (standardized to specific flavonoid and propolis content percentages), and propolis powder (spray-dried for ease of use in capsules, tablets, and topical formulations). Propolis extract concentrations are available from 30% up to 60%, with higher concentrations commanding premium prices. Chinese propolis is widely used in dietary supplements, throat sprays, oral care products, skincare formulations, and natural health remedies.

Bee Pollen

Bee pollen is a nutrient-dense superfood collected by honeybees from flowering plants. Chinese bee pollen is available in both granular (natural) and broken-cell-wall (powdered) forms. The broken-cell-wall process increases digestibility and nutrient absorption, making it the preferred choice for dietary supplement manufacturers. Common varieties include mixed wildflower pollen, rapeseed pollen, and lotus pollen. Bee pollen is rich in proteins (20-30%), amino acids, vitamins (especially B-complex), minerals, enzymes, and antioxidants. It is popular in health food products, smoothie mixes, and nutritional supplements.

Refined Beeswax

China is a major producer of refined beeswax, which undergoes filtration and purification to remove impurities while retaining its natural beneficial properties. Refined beeswax from China is used in cosmetics (lip balms, creams, salves), pharmaceuticals (coatings for tablets and capsules), food processing (coatings and glazes), candle making, leather treatment, and woodworking. It is available in slab, pellet, and pastille forms. Quality parameters include melting point (typically 62-65 degrees Celsius), color (white to pale yellow), free fatty acid content, and absence of residual pesticides or heavy metals. Organic refined beeswax is also available from certified producers.

5. Quality Standards & Certifications

Quality assurance is paramount when importing bee products, as these are natural food products subject to strict regulatory requirements in most destination markets. Understanding the key certifications and what they signify will help you evaluate suppliers and ensure compliance with your market's requirements.

HACCP (Hazard Analysis and Critical Control Points)

HACCP is an internationally recognized food safety management system that identifies, evaluates, and controls hazards throughout the production process. For bee products, HACCP certification demonstrates that a supplier has systematic controls in place for potential hazards including pesticide residues, heavy metal contamination, microbiological risks, and adulteration. Any serious bee product exporter should hold current HACCP certification from an accredited body.

ISO 9001

ISO 9001 certification indicates that a company operates a comprehensive quality management system covering all aspects of its operations, from procurement and production to packaging, storage, and customer service. While not specific to food safety, ISO 9001 demonstrates organizational discipline and a commitment to continuous improvement.

KOSHER and HALAL Certifications

KOSHER certification (from recognized rabbinical organizations) and HALAL certification (from accredited Islamic bodies) are essential for accessing Jewish and Muslim markets respectively. These certifications verify that products and production processes comply with religious dietary laws. For bee products, KOSHER certification requires verification that no non-KOSHER processing aids are used, while HALAL certification ensures no contamination with prohibited substances.

EU Organic and USDA NOP

Organic certifications confirm that bee products are produced without synthetic pesticides, antibiotics, or genetically modified organisms, and that hives are located in areas free from prohibited chemical exposure. EU Organic certification complies with European Union regulations (EC 834/2007), while USDA NOP (National Organic Program) certification meets United States organic standards. Both require rigorous annual inspections and detailed record-keeping. Organic bee products command premium prices and are increasingly demanded by health-conscious consumers worldwide.

What to Look for in Lab Tests

Beyond certifications, every shipment should be accompanied by a comprehensive laboratory analysis report (commonly called a Certificate of Analysis or COA). Key test parameters vary by product:

  • Honey: Moisture content (below 20%), HMF (below 40 mg/kg for most markets), diastase activity, sugar profile, pesticide residues, antibiotic residues (especially chloramphenicol, nitrofurans, and tetracyclines), heavy metals (lead, cadmium, arsenic, mercury), and C4 sugar content (to detect adulteration with cane or corn syrup).
  • Royal Jelly: 10-HDA content (minimum 1.4% for premium grade), moisture content, protein content, sugar profile, and absence of antibiotic residues.
  • Propolis: Total flavonoid content, propolis content percentage, lead and heavy metal levels, and residue solvent levels (for extracted propolis).
  • Bee Pollen: Moisture content, protein content, total aerobic plate count, yeast and mold counts, and pesticide residues.
  • Beeswax: Melting point, ester value, saponification value, acid value, color, and absence of foreign substances.

Key Takeaways: Quality Standards

  • HACCP: Food safety management system; essential for any serious exporter.
  • ISO 9001: Quality management system demonstrating organizational discipline.
  • KOSHER / HALAL: Required for Jewish and Muslim market access.
  • EU Organic / NOP: Needed for organic product claims in Europe and the US.
  • Certificate of Analysis (COA): Every shipment should include lab test results for moisture, HMF, antibiotics, heavy metals, and other key parameters.

6. Import Regulations & Compliance

Bee products are classified as food products in most jurisdictions and are subject to specific import regulations that vary significantly by destination country. Non-compliance can result in shipment rejection, detention, destruction, or costly re-export, making it essential to understand and comply with all applicable requirements before placing orders.

United States (FDA)

The U.S. Food and Drug Administration regulates bee products as food. Key requirements include: registration of the foreign supplier through the FDA's Foreign Supplier Verification Programs (FSVP), compliance with the Food Safety Modernization Act (FSMA), proper labeling in accordance with the Food, Drug, and Cosmetic Act (including allergen declarations), adherence to maximum residue limits (MRLs) for pesticides and antibiotics (the FDA maintains a zero-tolerance policy for chloramphenicol in honey), and prior notice of shipment through the FDA's Prior Notice System. Honey imported into the U.S. must also comply with honey grading standards established by the USDA if labeled with USDA grade marks.

European Union

The EU has some of the most stringent regulations for bee product imports. Requirements include: compliance with EU Regulation 2017/625 on official controls, mandatory health certificates issued by the competent authority in the exporting country, adherence to EU MRLs for pesticides (Regulation EC 396/2005), compliance with maximum levels for heavy metals and contaminants (Regulation EC 1881/2006), honey must meet the Codex Alimentarius standard (Codex STAN 12-1981), and products labeled as organic must be accompanied by a valid certificate of inspection from an EU-recognized control body under Regulation EU 2018/848. The EU also requires traceability throughout the supply chain.

Canada (CFIA)

The Canadian Food Inspection Agency regulates bee product imports under the Safe Food for Canadians Act (SFCA) and the Food and Drugs Act. Importers must hold a valid SFC license, provide a Safe Food for Canadians import license number, and ensure products meet Canadian standards. Honey must comply with the Canadian honey standard (which requires a minimum of 60% reducing sugars and maximum 20% moisture). Canada also enforces strict limits on antibiotic residues and pesticides.

Australia (TGA / DAFF)

Bee products imported into Australia are regulated by the Department of Agriculture, Fisheries and Forestry (DAFF) and, for therapeutic goods, the Therapeutic Goods Administration (TGA). Importers must obtain a valid import permit, comply with Australia's biosecurity requirements (including treatment or inspection if required), meet the requirements of the Imported Food Control Order, and ensure products conform to the Australia New Zealand Food Standards Code. Australia has particularly strict biosecurity rules regarding bee products due to concerns about exotic bee diseases.

Essential Documentation

Regardless of the destination market, the following documents are typically required for bee product imports:

  • Commercial Invoice: Detailed description of products, quantities, unit prices, total value, and payment terms.
  • Packing List: Itemized list of all packages, weights, and dimensions.
  • Bill of Lading (B/L) or Airway Bill (AWB): Transport document issued by the shipping carrier.
  • Certificate of Origin: Issued by a chamber of commerce or authorized body, confirming the country of origin.
  • Health Certificate: Issued by the exporting country's food safety authority, confirming the products are fit for human consumption and meet the importing country's requirements.
  • Phytosanitary Certificate: Required by some countries for bee products, certifying that the products are free from specified pests and diseases.
  • Certificate of Analysis (COA): Laboratory test results confirming product specifications and compliance with quality parameters.
  • Certification Copies: Copies of relevant certifications (HACCP, Organic, KOSHER, HALAL, etc.).
  • Insurance Certificate: Proof of cargo insurance coverage during transit.

7. Sampling & Quality Testing

Never commit to a large order without first evaluating product samples. A structured sampling and testing protocol protects you from quality issues, adulteration, and regulatory non-compliance.

How to Request Samples

When contacting potential suppliers, request samples of the specific products and grades you intend to purchase. Best practices include:

  • Request samples from at least 3-5 different suppliers for comparison.
  • Ask for production-run samples (not specially prepared samples) to get a realistic representation of what you will receive in bulk orders.
  • Request a minimum of 500g to 1kg per sample for honey, and appropriate quantities for other products.
  • Ensure samples are shipped in the same packaging that would be used for bulk orders.
  • For royal jelly, request both fresh (frozen) and freeze-dried samples if both forms are of interest.
  • Ask the supplier to include their standard Certificate of Analysis with each sample.

Third-Party Testing

While supplier-provided COAs are important, independent third-party testing provides an objective verification of product quality. In the bee products industry, the most commonly used international testing laboratories include:

  • Intertek: A global quality and safety services provider offering comprehensive testing for food products, including honey authenticity analysis, antibiotic residue screening, and pesticide detection.
  • Eurofins: A leading life sciences company specializing in food, environmental, pharmaceutical, and cosmetics testing, with advanced capabilities for detecting honey adulteration and residue analysis.

Other internationally recognized testing organizations such as SGS and Bureau Veritas also offer food and agricultural product testing services. When selecting a laboratory, confirm that they have specific experience with bee products and can perform the exact tests required by your destination market's regulations.

Key Test Parameters by Product

When commissioning third-party tests, specify the following parameters based on the product type:

  • Honey: Pollen analysis (to verify botanical and geographical origin), C4 sugar test (isotope ratio mass spectrometry to detect syrup adulteration), antibiotic residue screening (multi-residue LC-MS/MS), pesticide residue screening, HMF and diastase activity, moisture content, and microbiological analysis.
  • Royal Jelly: 10-HDA content by HPLC, moisture content, protein content, antibiotic residues, and microbiological analysis.
  • Propolis: Total flavonoid content (UV-Vis spectrophotometry), lead content (ICP-MS), and residual solvent analysis (GC-MS for ethanol or glycol extraction residues).
  • Bee Pollen: Moisture, protein, total aerobic count, yeast and mold, and pesticide multi-residue screening.
  • Beeswax: Melting point, ester value, saponification value, acid value, and hydrocarbon content (to detect paraffin adulteration).

Key Takeaways: Sampling & Quality Testing

  • Sample Comparison: Request production-run samples from 3-5 suppliers for realistic evaluation.
  • Third-Party Labs: Always rely on verified international labs like Intertek or Eurofins for independent testing.
  • Critical Testing: Prioritize pollen purity, 10-HDA levels, C4 sugars, and antibiotic residue screening.

8. Shipping & Logistics

Proper logistics planning is crucial for bee products, as some products (particularly royal jelly) are temperature-sensitive, and all products require appropriate packaging to maintain quality during international transit.

Sea Freight vs. Air Freight

Sea freight is the most cost-effective option for large-volume shipments and is the standard method for honey, beeswax, bee pollen, and propolis. Transit times from China to major destinations typically range from 15-35 days depending on the port of origin and destination. A full container load (FCL) of honey in 290kg drums typically consists of 80 drums (approximately 23,200 kg) in a 20-foot container. Less-than-container load (LCL) shipments are available for smaller quantities but carry a higher per-unit cost and slightly higher risk of damage.

Air freight is significantly more expensive but much faster (typically 3-7 days). It is recommended for high-value, time-sensitive, or temperature-critical shipments such as fresh royal jelly, small sample orders, and urgent replenishment orders. Air freight costs are calculated by volumetric or actual weight, whichever is greater.

Packaging Requirements

  • Honey: Standard export packaging is 290kg (net weight) food-grade steel drums with internal food-safe coating. Smaller containers (25kg pails or 1kg retail jars) are available upon request.
  • Royal Jelly (fresh): Must be shipped frozen at -18 degrees Celsius or below. Typically packed in 1kg or 5kg food-grade plastic containers, then packed in insulated cartons with dry ice or gel packs for air freight, or in refrigerated (reefer) containers for sea freight.
  • Royal Jelly (freeze-dried): Packaged in sealed foil pouches or drums with desiccant. No cold chain required; shelf life of 24+ months when stored properly.
  • Propolis: Raw blocks in cartons, extract in sealed drums or IBC totes, powder in sealed foil bags or fiber drums with inner liner.
  • Bee Pollen: Packed in sealed foil bags or food-grade fiber drums. For long-distance sea freight, nitrogen-flushed packaging helps prevent oxidation and maintain freshness.
  • Beeswax: Typically packed in 25kg or 50kg cartons (slab form) or bags (pellet/pastille form).

Temperature Control Considerations

Fresh royal jelly is the most temperature-sensitive bee product and requires a continuous cold chain from production through delivery. For sea freight, a refrigerated container (reefer) set to -18 degrees Celsius is mandatory. For air freight, insulated packaging with sufficient cold packs or dry ice is required, and the shipment should be booked on the fastest available routing to minimize time in transit. Even products that do not require refrigeration, such as honey and bee pollen, should be protected from extreme heat during summer months, as prolonged exposure to high temperatures can degrade quality parameters.

Shelf Life Considerations

  • Honey: Virtually indefinite shelf life when stored properly (cool, dry, dark conditions). Export honey typically carries a 24-month best-before date.
  • Fresh Royal Jelly: 18-24 months when stored frozen at -18 degrees Celsius. Thawed product should be consumed within a few weeks.
  • Freeze-Dried Royal Jelly: 24-36 months at room temperature in sealed, moisture-proof packaging.
  • Propolis Extract: 24+ months when stored in sealed containers away from light and heat.
  • Bee Pollen: 12-18 months when stored in cool, dry conditions. Refrigeration extends shelf life.
  • Beeswax: Indefinite shelf life when stored in cool, dry conditions away from direct sunlight.

Incoterms

Incoterms (International Commercial Terms) define the responsibilities, costs, and risks between buyer and seller in international trade. Common Incoterms for bee product imports from China include:

  • FOB (Free On Board): The seller delivers goods to the port of shipment (e.g., Shanghai or Ningbo). The buyer is responsible for ocean freight, insurance, and all costs from the port of loading onward. This is the most commonly used term for sea freight.
  • CIF (Cost, Insurance, and Freight): The seller pays for ocean freight and insurance to the port of destination. The buyer handles import clearance and onward transport.
  • EXW (Ex Works): The seller makes goods available at their premises. The buyer bears all costs and risks from that point onward.
  • DDP (Delivered Duty Paid): The seller is responsible for all costs including import duties and taxes to deliver goods to the buyer's specified location. This is the most seller-burdensome term and less common for bulk commodity imports.

Key Takeaways: Shipping & Logistics

  • Sea Freight: Cost-effective for bulk; 15-35 days transit. FCL honey = ~80 drums (23,200 kg) per 20ft container.
  • Air Freight: 3-7 days; use for fresh royal jelly, samples, and urgent orders.
  • Fresh Royal Jelly: Requires continuous cold chain at -18°C (reefer container or insulated packaging with dry ice).
  • Common Incoterms: FOB (most common), CIF, EXW, DDP.
  • Shelf Life: Honey indefinite; freeze-dried royal jelly 24-36 months; propolis 24+ months; pollen 12-18 months.

9. Payment Terms & Risk Management

Establishing secure payment terms is critical to protecting your financial interests when importing from China. Bee product imports typically involve significant capital, and the international nature of the transaction adds layers of complexity and risk.

Common Payment Methods

  • Telegraphic Transfer (T/T): The most widely used payment method in China trade. The standard arrangement for initial orders is typically 30% deposit upon order confirmation, with 70% balance paid before shipment or upon receipt of a copy of the Bill of Lading. For established relationships with proven suppliers, more flexible terms may be negotiated. T/T is fast (typically 2-5 business days) and has relatively low bank fees, but offers limited recourse if something goes wrong.
  • Letter of Credit (L/C): An L/C is a commitment by the buyer's bank to pay the seller upon presentation of specified documents (such as the Bill of Lading, Certificate of Analysis, and commercial invoice). L/Cs provide strong protection for both parties but involve higher bank fees (typically 0.5-2% of the transaction value) and more complex documentation requirements. They are recommended for very large orders or when working with a new supplier for the first time.
  • PayPal / Escrow: Suitable for small sample orders or initial trial orders (typically under $5,000). These platforms offer buyer protection but are not practical for large commercial shipments due to high transaction fees and transfer limits.

Payment Protection Strategies

When working with international suppliers, protecting your payment is critical. Several strategies can reduce your financial risk:

  • Escrow Services: Some B2B platforms and independent escrow providers hold funds until both parties confirm satisfactory delivery and quality. This provides a neutral third-party safeguard for initial transactions.
  • Documentary Collections: Under a documentary collection arrangement, your bank releases payment to the supplier only upon presentation of specified shipping documents (such as the Bill of Lading and Certificate of Analysis). This offers more protection than open T/T while being less expensive than a full Letter of Credit.
  • Staged Payments: Structure payments in stages tied to verifiable milestones: deposit upon order confirmation, progress payment after production completion (supported by photos or inspection reports), and final payment upon receipt of shipping documents or after pre-shipment inspection.
  • Third-Party Inspection Before Final Payment: Arrange for an independent inspection company to verify product quality, quantity, and packaging before releasing the final payment. This is one of the most effective ways to ensure you receive what you ordered.

How to Protect Yourself

  • Never pay 100% upfront to a new supplier, regardless of how attractive the pricing may be. A 30% deposit is standard industry practice.
  • Use formal contracts that clearly specify product specifications, quality parameters, packaging, delivery timeline, payment terms, inspection rights, and dispute resolution mechanisms.
  • Include quality clauses in your contract that allow you to reject or renegotiate payment if products fail to meet agreed specifications upon independent testing.
  • Consider using a third-party inspection company to conduct a pre-shipment inspection (PSI) before releasing final payment.
  • Build relationships gradually: Start with smaller trial orders and increase volume as trust and confidence in the supplier grow.
  • Verify bank account details independently (call the supplier's bank using a number you find yourself, not one provided in an email) to prevent fraud.

Key Takeaways: Payment & Risk Management

  • T/T: Most common; 30% deposit, 70% balance before shipment or against B/L copy.
  • L/C: Best for large orders or new suppliers; higher fees (0.5-2%) but strong protection.
  • Never pay 100% upfront to a new supplier.
  • Protect yourself: Use formal contracts, include quality clauses, verify bank details independently, and conduct pre-shipment inspections.

10. Step-by-Step Import Process

Here is a practical, numbered guide to take you from initial research to receiving your bee product shipment at your destination.

  1. Define Your Requirements: Determine exactly which products you need, in what quantities, to what quality specifications, and for which target market. Research your destination country's import regulations and identify all required certifications and documentation.
  2. Research and Identify Suppliers: Use the channels described in Section 4 (direct contact via official websites, industry referrals, and trade publications) to compile a shortlist of 5-10 potential suppliers that match your requirements.
  3. Request Samples and Quotations: Contact your shortlisted suppliers with detailed inquiries. Request product samples, technical specifications, certificates, pricing (FOB and CIF), minimum order quantities, lead times, and payment terms.
  4. Evaluate Samples: Conduct sensory evaluation (taste, aroma, color, texture) and send samples to an independent laboratory for comprehensive testing as described in Section 7.
  5. Verify Supplier Credentials: Independently verify business licenses, certifications, export licenses, and references as outlined in Section 4.
  6. Negotiate Terms and Sign Contract: Select your preferred supplier and negotiate final terms including price, quantity, quality parameters, packaging, delivery schedule, payment terms, Incoterms, and dispute resolution. Execute a formal purchase contract.
  7. Pay Deposit: Remit the agreed deposit (typically 30-50%) via T/T or open an L/C if applicable.
  8. Production and Pre-Shipment Inspection: The supplier produces your order. Arrange for a pre-shipment inspection by a third-party inspection company (Intertek, Eurofins, or Bureau Veritas) to verify product quality, quantity, and packaging before shipment.
  9. Prepare Import Documentation: Work with your customs broker to prepare all required import documentation, including import permits, food safety registrations, and any market-specific requirements.
  10. Shipping: The supplier arranges shipment per the agreed Incoterms. Monitor the shipment progress and coordinate with your freight forwarder for arrival at the destination port.
  11. Customs Clearance and Payment of Balance: Clear the shipment through customs, pay applicable duties and taxes, and release the balance payment (if applicable under your payment terms).
  12. Receive and Inspect Goods: Take delivery of the shipment, inspect goods upon arrival, and conduct any additional quality testing if required. Report any discrepancies to the supplier promptly.
  13. Build the Relationship: Provide feedback to your supplier, discuss future orders, and work toward building a long-term partnership that benefits both parties.

11. Common Mistakes to Avoid

Learning from the mistakes of others can save you significant time, money, and frustration. Here are the most common pitfalls that bee product buyers encounter when importing from China.

  1. Choosing Suppliers Based Solely on Price: The cheapest quote is rarely the best value. Extremely low prices often indicate inferior quality, adulterated products, or unreliable suppliers. Always prioritize quality, certifications, and track record over price alone. A slightly higher price from a reputable supplier will save you far more in the long run through consistent quality, reliable delivery, and regulatory compliance.
  2. Skip Independent Testing: Relying solely on supplier-provided Certificates of Analysis is a significant risk. Some suppliers may provide inaccurate or falsified test results. Always commission independent third-party testing, especially for new suppliers and first orders. The cost of testing (typically $200-500 per sample) is negligible compared to the cost of a rejected shipment or a product recall.
  3. Ignoring Regulatory Requirements: Failing to research and comply with your destination country's import regulations is one of the most expensive mistakes a buyer can make. Shipments that do not meet regulatory requirements may be detained, destroyed, or re-exported at your expense. Always verify requirements with your national food safety authority and a qualified customs broker before placing orders.
  4. Ordering Large Quantities Without a Trial: Placing a large first order without first conducting a trial order and thorough quality evaluation is extremely risky. Start with a manageable trial order (typically 1-3 pallets or one drum of each product) to validate quality, packaging, documentation, and the supplier's reliability before committing to container-load quantities.
  5. Poor Communication and Vague Specifications: Ambiguous product specifications, quality requirements, and packaging instructions lead to misunderstandings, disputes, and disappointment. Provide your supplier with detailed, written specifications for every aspect of your order, including product grade, quality parameters, packaging type and dimensions, labeling requirements, and delivery schedule. Use photographs and reference samples where possible.
  6. Overlooking Logistics and Cold Chain Requirements: Failing to plan for temperature-sensitive products (especially fresh royal jelly) can result in complete product loss during transit. Always specify and verify cold chain requirements, packaging specifications, and transit times before shipping. For fresh royal jelly, insist on temperature loggers in the shipment to verify that the cold chain was maintained throughout transit.
  7. Not Building Long-Term Relationships: Treating every transaction as a one-off deal prevents you from building the trust and mutual understanding that lead to better pricing, priority production scheduling, and more flexible terms over time. Invest in building genuine business relationships with your suppliers through regular communication, fair dealing, and constructive feedback.

Key Takeaways: Common Mistakes to Avoid

  • Don't choose suppliers based solely on price — prioritize quality, certifications, and track record.
  • Never skip independent third-party testing, even if the supplier provides a COA.
  • Research your destination country's import regulations before placing any orders.
  • Always start with a trial order before committing to container-load quantities.
  • Provide detailed, written specifications for every aspect of your order.
  • Plan cold chain logistics for temperature-sensitive products (especially fresh royal jelly).
  • Invest in long-term supplier relationships for better pricing and priority scheduling.
