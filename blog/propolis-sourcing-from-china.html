1. What Is Propolis and Why Is It in Demand?

Propolis is a resinous substance collected by honeybees (Apis mellifera) from the buds, bark, sap flows and other botanical sources of various trees and plants. Bees use propolis to seal small gaps and cracks in the hive, reinforce the structural integrity of honeycomb, sterilise the interior surfaces of the hive, and protect the colony against bacteria, fungi and other microorganisms. The word "propolis" itself derives from the Greek "pro" (before, or at the entrance of) and "polis" (city), a reference to its role as a defensive barrier guarding the entrance of the bee community.

The composition of propolis is remarkably complex and varies significantly depending on the geographical region, local flora and seasonal conditions. A single sample of propolis may contain more than 300 individual chemical compounds, including resins, waxes, essential oils, pollen and a wide array of bioactive substances. It is these bioactive constituents that drive the commercial demand for propolis across multiple global industries.

Key Bioactive Compounds

The therapeutic and commercial value of propolis is primarily attributed to several classes of bioactive compounds that are consistently present in high-quality material regardless of geographic origin:

  • Flavonoids: the most important class of compounds in propolis, including galangin, chrysin, pinocembrin, pinobanksin and quercetin. Flavonoids are directly responsible for propolis's well-documented antioxidant, anti-inflammatory and antimicrobial properties. They are the primary basis for quality grading and are the compound class most commonly quantified on Certificates of Analysis.
  • Phenolic acids: including caffeic acid, ferulic acid and p-coumaric acid, which contribute additional antioxidant activity and support immune-modulating functions. These compounds work synergistically with flavonoids to enhance the overall bioactivity of propolis.
  • Terpenes: a diverse class of volatile organic compounds that provide antibacterial and antifungal properties. Terpenes contribute to the characteristic aromatic profile of propolis and play a role in its natural preservative function within the hive.
  • Caffeic acid phenethyl ester (CAPE): one of the most extensively studied individual compounds in propolis, CAPE has demonstrated anti-inflammatory, antiviral, immunomodulatory and potential anticancer properties in numerous scientific studies. CAPE content is frequently used as a quality marker for pharmaceutical-grade propolis and is typically quantified using HPLC analysis.
  • Waxes and resins: structural components that form the base matrix of propolis, contributing to its physical properties including hardness, pliability and melting point. While not bioactive in the same way as flavonoids, waxes are a natural and expected component of raw propolis.

Among these compound classes, flavonoid content is universally recognised as the single most important quality indicator for commercial propolis. Propolis content itself — the total proportion of propolis material excluding wax, impurities and moisture — serves as the overarching quality differentiator between product grades, with higher propolis content indicating greater concentration of bioactive compounds.

  • Oral care products: propolis's natural antibacterial activity against common oral pathogens — including Streptococcus mutans, the primary bacteria responsible for dental caries — has made it a valued ingredient in natural toothpaste, mouthwash and dental care products. Its ability to support gum health, reduce oral bacterial load and soothe oral inflammation is well supported by clinical research.
  • Pharmaceutical raw materials: ongoing research into propolis's bioactive compounds, particularly CAPE and specific flavonoids such as galangin and pinocembrin, continues to expand its pharmaceutical applications. 2. Types of Propolis Available from China

When sourcing propolis from Chinese suppliers, buyers will encounter three primary solid-form products, each designed for specific applications and processing requirements. All three types are solid at room temperature and do not require cold chain logistics. Understanding the differences between these forms — particularly the distinction in propolis content and degree of processing — is essential for selecting the right product for your market and formulation needs.

2.1 Refined Propolis (Purified, up to 99% Propolis Content)

Refined propolis is the highest purity grade of propolis available in solid form. It is produced through a specialised purification process in which raw propolis is processed to remove the majority of beeswax, impurities, bee debris and other non-propolis material. The result is a highly concentrated solid propolis product with a propolis content that can reach 99%, making it the most potent form available for buyers who require maximum bioactive compound concentration.

The purification process for refined propolis involves several stages of physical and chemical separation. Raw propolis is first cleaned of visible debris, then subjected to controlled processing that selectively removes wax and impurities while preserving the full spectrum of bioactive flavonoids, phenolic acids, terpenes and other valuable compounds. The final product is a solid material, typically supplied in blocks or pieces, with minimal residual wax content — generally 2% or less.

Key characteristics of refined propolis include:

  • Form: solid blocks or pieces, typically dark brown to near-black in colour, with a dense, resinous texture and concentrated aromatic profile
  • Propolis content: can reach 99%, representing the highest purity grade available in solid propolis. Typical commercial specifications target a range of 95-99%.
  • Wax content: ≤2%, as the vast majority of beeswax has been removed during the purification process
  • Flavonoid content: high, reflecting the concentrated nature of the purified material. The removal of wax and inert material means that flavonoids are present at significantly higher concentrations than in raw or cleaned propolis on a per-gram basis.
  • Moisture: ≤5% to ensure stability during storage and transport

2.2 Clean Propolis Blocks (55-60% Propolis Content)

Clean propolis blocks are raw propolis that has been physically cleaned to remove bee debris, wood chips, sand and other visible impurities, but has not undergone the purification process used to produce refined propolis. As a result, clean propolis blocks retain the natural beeswax content that is present in the raw material as collected from the hive. The propolis content is approximately 55-60%, with the balance consisting primarily of natural beeswax along with small amounts of pollen and other hive-derived materials.

Clean propolis blocks are the most cost-effective option per kilogram among the three product types, making them an attractive choice for buyers who have the capability to perform further processing in-house or who have applications where some wax content is acceptable. The cleaning process involves physical separation, washing and sorting to produce a raw material that is free of visible debris while preserving the natural composition of the propolis as much as possible.

Key characteristics of clean propolis blocks include:

  • Form: solid blocks, typically dark brown to black in colour, with a characteristic resinous texture and natural beeswax aroma. The blocks have a somewhat softer consistency compared to refined propolis due to the higher wax content.
  • Propolis content: approximately 55-60%, with the balance being natural beeswax and trace materials
  • Wax content: 25-45%, representing the natural wax content retained from the raw material
  • Flavonoid content: moderate, reflecting the dilution effect of the natural beeswax content
  • Moisture: ≤8% to ensure stability during storage and transport
  • Impurity level: low, with visible debris, bee parts, wood chips and foreign matter removed during the cleaning process

The powder form offers significant practical advantages in manufacturing environments. Why Propolis Content Is the Primary Quality Differentiator

Unlike some propolis products on the market that are standardised primarily by flavonoid content, the three solid-form products described in this guide are differentiated principally by their total propolis content. Propolis content represents the total proportion of genuine propolis material in the product, excluding wax, moisture and physical impurities. It is the most direct indicator of how much bioactive material you are receiving per kilogram purchased.

For refined propolis, the propolis content of 95-99% means that nearly every gram of material you receive is active propolis. For clean propolis blocks at 55-60%, approximately 40-45% of each kilogram consists of natural beeswax, which has its own value but does not contribute the flavonoids and bioactive compounds associated with propolis. For propolis powder at up to 60%, the propolis content is similar to clean blocks but in a more convenient powdered form.

Understanding this distinction helps buyers make informed purchasing decisions. If your formulation requires maximum propolis potency and minimal wax, refined propolis is the appropriate choice despite its higher per-kilogram cost, because the effective cost per gram of actual propolis content may be competitive. If you perform your own extraction, clean blocks at 55-60% propolis content may be more economical, as the wax will be separated during your extraction process anyway.

Flavonoid Content as Secondary Quality Marker

While propolis content tells you how much propolis is in the product, flavonoid content tells you how potent that propolis is. Flavonoids — including galangin, chrysin, pinocembrin and other compounds — are directly responsible for the antioxidant, anti-inflammatory and antimicrobial properties that drive commercial demand for propolis. Flavonoid content is therefore an important secondary quality marker that complements the propolis content figure.

Flavonoid content is typically measured using spectrophotometric methods, most commonly the aluminium chloride colorimetric method, which provides a reliable total flavonoid value expressed as a percentage by weight. For more detailed characterisation, buyers may request HPLC (high-performance liquid chromatography) profiling to quantify individual flavonoid compounds. When evaluating flavonoid data on a COA, always verify the testing method used, as different methodologies can yield different numerical results — consistency in the testing method across batches is essential for meaningful quality comparison.

Heavy Metal and Pesticide Testing for Import Compliance

Heavy metal testing is a critical safety parameter for propolis regardless of the product form. Bees collect resin from trees that may be growing in environments with varying levels of soil or atmospheric contamination, making heavy metal screening an essential part of every COA. The four heavy metals most commonly tested are lead (Pb), arsenic (As), cadmium (Cd) and mercury (Hg).

Buyers importing propolis into the European Union must ensure that heavy metal levels comply with EU regulations, which set specific maximum limits for these contaminants in food supplements and food ingredients. The United States Pharmacopeia (USP) publishes heavy metal limits that are widely referenced in the US dietary supplement industry. For markets with stringent regulatory requirements — including the EU, US, Japan, South Korea and Australia — heavy metal testing should be a non-negotiable part of every COA.

Pesticide residue testing is equally important. Propolis should be screened for a panel of common pesticides, and the COA should report results as either non-detectable (ND) or within the applicable maximum residue limits for the destination market. When evaluating both heavy metal and pesticide data, look not only for compliance with limits but also for consistency across batches — wide fluctuations between batches may indicate inconsistent raw material sourcing or inadequate quality control at the factory level.

CAPE Testing for Pharmaceutical-Grade Requirements

Caffeic acid phenethyl ester (CAPE) is one of the most extensively studied individual compounds in propolis. It has demonstrated anti-inflammatory, antiviral, immunomodulatory and potential anticancer properties in numerous scientific studies. For buyers sourcing propolis for pharmaceutical applications, CAPE content is often tested as an additional quality parameter beyond the standard COA items.

CAPE is typically quantified using HPLC analysis, which can detect and measure specific compounds at very low concentrations. While not all buyers require CAPE testing — most dietary supplement and cosmetic applications do not need it — CAPE testing is increasingly requested by pharmaceutical companies and research institutions that need precise characterisation of their propolis raw materials. If your application requires CAPE testing, discuss this requirement with your supplier at the quotation stage, as additional testing may affect pricing and lead time.

Manufacturing Standards

  • HACCP (Hazard Analysis and Critical Control Points): this is the internationally recognised food safety management system. HACCP certification demonstrates that the manufacturing facility has identified potential hazards at each stage of production — from raw material receiving through processing, packaging and storage — and implemented preventive controls to eliminate or reduce these hazards to acceptable levels. For buyers sourcing propolis for human consumption, whether for supplements, foods or pharmaceuticals, HACCP certification should be considered a baseline requirement.
  • ISO 9001: this certification confirms that the manufacturing facility operates a documented quality management system (QMS) covering all aspects of production, from raw material procurement through processing, testing, packaging and delivery. ISO 9001 certification indicates systematic quality control, documented procedures, internal audit processes and a commitment to continuous improvement. It provides buyers with confidence that the factory maintains consistent quality standards across production batches.

Religious Dietary Certifications

  • KOSHER: required for products distributed in Jewish markets or sold to consumers who observe Kosher dietary laws. KOSHER certification for propolis involves verification that no non-Kosher substances are used in the production process and that the manufacturing equipment meets Kosher requirements. This certification is issued by recognised Kosher certification bodies that conduct periodic audits of the manufacturing facility.
  • HALAL: required for products distributed in Muslim-majority markets or sold to consumers who require HALAL compliance. HALAL certification confirms that the propolis and all processing aids comply with Islamic dietary requirements. Both KOSHER and HALAL certifications are important market access credentials for buyers serving diverse global consumer bases.

Organic Certifications

  • EU Organic: required for products sold in the European Union that carry the organic label. This certification requires that the propolis is sourced from hives located in areas that meet organic beekeeping standards, with restrictions on chemical treatments and specific requirements for hive management, forage area and surrounding land use.
  • USDA NOP (National Organic Program): required for products sold in the United States that carry the USDA Organic seal. Similar to EU Organic in its fundamental requirements, USDA NOP certification for propolis involves compliance with organic apiculture standards, chain-of-custody documentation and annual inspections by accredited certification agencies.

Import Regulations by Market

  • European Union: propolis intended for use in food supplements must comply with EU food supplement directive requirements. Heavy metal limits, microbiological standards and labelling requirements must be met for customs clearance. Depending on the specific product form and intended use, EU novel food considerations may apply — buyers should verify the regulatory status of propolis in their specific product category.
  • United States: under US FDA regulations, propolis sold as a dietary supplement ingredient must comply with current Good Manufacturing Practice (cGMP) requirements. The FDA classifies propolis as a dietary ingredient, and products containing propolis must meet labelling, safety and manufacturing standards set forth in the Dietary Supplement Health and Education Act (DSHEA).

7. Common Buyer Questions (FAQ)

Q1: What is the difference between refined propolis and clean propolis?

Refined propolis has been purified to remove wax and impurities, achieving propolis content up to 99%. It is the highest purity grade available in solid form and is suited for pharmaceutical raw materials and high-end cosmetic formulations where maximum potency and minimal wax content are required. Clean propolis retains natural beeswax content with propolis content around 55-60%, making it more affordable per kilogram and suitable for buyers who perform their own extraction or for applications where some wax content is acceptable. The key distinction is the degree of processing and the resulting propolis-to-wax ratio.

Q2: Is the propolis powder water-soluble?

No. The propolis powder offered is standard ground propolis powder, not water-soluble. It retains the natural hydrophobic properties of propolis and will not dissolve readily in water. The powder is designed for direct encapsulation into capsules, tablet pressing alongside other ingredients, or incorporation into dry formulations and cosmetic powders where water solubility is not required. If your application requires water-soluble propolis, you would need to perform your own extraction or processing on clean propolis blocks or refined propolis to produce a water-compatible form.

Q3: What propolis content should I choose for dietary supplement capsules?

For capsules where propolis is the primary active ingredient and you want to maximise the potency per capsule, refined propolis with 95-99% propolis content delivers the highest concentration of bioactive flavonoids and other compounds per unit of material. This allows you to either achieve a higher dosage in a standard capsule size or use a smaller capsule while maintaining your target dosage. Propolis powder with up to approximately 60% propolis content offers a good balance of potency and cost for multi-ingredient formulations where propolis is one of several active ingredients. The powder form is also more convenient for standard encapsulation equipment. Your choice ultimately depends on your formulation goals, dosage targets and cost considerations.
